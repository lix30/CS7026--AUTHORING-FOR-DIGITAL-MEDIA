Marciana Library

Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana
Listen to guide

Story

The Marciana Library (Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana) in Venice is one of the oldest and most important libraries in Italy, known for its vast collection of classical texts and stunning Renaissance architecture.

Founded in the 16th century, it was envisioned as a grand repository for knowledge, fulfilling the wishes of Cardinal Bessarion, who donated his vast collection of Greek and Latin manuscripts to Venice in 1468. Construction began in 1537 under the renowned architect Jacopo Sansovino, and the library became a masterpiece of Venetian Renaissance design.

Located in Piazza San Marco, near the Doge’s Palace, it houses rare manuscripts, ancient maps, and works by famous scholars. Over the centuries, it has been a center of learning and culture, preserving Venice’s intellectual heritage. Today, the Marciana Library remains a symbol of Venetian scholarship and artistic achievement.

Facts

A Gift of Knowledge – The library was founded after Cardinal Bessarion, a Greek scholar and former Archbishop of Constantinople, donated over 1,000 rare Greek and Latin manuscripts to Venice in 1468 to preserve classical knowledge.

Designed by a Renaissance Master – The library’s stunning architecture was created by Jacopo Sansovino, one of the greatest Renaissance architects, who also designed parts of St. Mark’s Square.

Home to Rare Treasures – Among its priceless collections, the library holds a 15th-century edition of Ptolemy’s "Geography," early printed books, and the world’s oldest surviving complete manuscript of Homer’s "Iliad."

A Secret Door – The library has a hidden passageway connecting it to the Doge’s Palace, allowing secret movement between the two buildings.

A Center of Learning for Centuries – The library continues to be a major center for historical and academic research, holding over a million books, including rare manuscripts and maps from Venice’s golden age.

Characters

Cardinal Bessarion (1403–1472) – The Library’s Founder

A Greek scholar and former Archbishop of Constantinople, Bessarion donated over 1,000 manuscripts in 1468 to preserve ancient Greek and Latin knowledge after the fall of Constantinople in 1453. His collection became the foundation of the Marciana Library.

Jacopo Sansovino (1486–1570) – The Architect

One of Venice’s greatest Renaissance architects, Sansovino designed the stunning library building in 1537. His work set a new standard for Venetian Renaissance architecture.

Titian (c. 1488–1576) – The Painter

The renowned Venetian painter Titian contributed to the library’s decoration, creating some of the ceiling frescoes.

Galileo Galilei (1564–1642) – The Astronomer

Galileo gave public lectures at the Marciana Library in the early 1600s, where he demonstrated his telescope and presented his astronomical discoveries. The Venetian Republic was one of the first governments to recognize the potential of his work.

Tomaso Giannotti (16th Century) – The First Librarian

Appointed in 1560, he became the first official librarian of the Marciana Library and helped organize its growing collection.

Elisabetta Caminer Turra (1751–1796) – A Trailblazing Female Scholar

A pioneering Venetian female writer and publisher, Turra used the Marciana Library for research and is considered one of the first professional female journalists in Italy.

Floor/Area Guide

Ground Floor – The Entrance & Public Areas

Sansovino’s Architecture: Admire the elegant Renaissance-style façade designed by Jacopo Sansovino in the 16th century.

Statues & Sculptures: The entrance is decorated with marble sculptures, including works by Alessandro Vittoria.

Bookshop & Information Desk: Visitors can find historical books, guides, and souvenirs.

First Floor – Monumental Rooms & Artworks

Sala Sansoviniana (Sansovino Hall): A breathtaking hall with frescoes by Titian, Veronese, and Tintoretto. The ceiling celebrates wisdom and knowledge with paintings of ancient philosophers and gods.

Antique Globes & Maps: Large 16th-century terrestrial and celestial globes showcase Renaissance-era cartography.

Rare Manuscripts & Books: Some of the oldest books and manuscripts from the library’s collection are displayed, including ancient Greek and Latin texts.

Second Floor – Reading Rooms & Research Areas (Restricted Access)

Scholars’ Reading Room: This is where researchers and academics can study original manuscripts and rare books.

Historical Archives: A collection of Venice’s state documents, historical maps, and scientific texts.

Ptolemy’s Geography: A famous early printed edition of Ptolemy’s maps, one of the first modern atlases.

Accessibility Icons & info here

The Marciana Library in Venice is a historical and architectural gem, but its age and location present some challenges for accessibility. Here's what you need to know:

Accessibility for Wheelchair Users

Entrance Ramp: The main entrance has a ramp that makes the library accessible for wheelchair users. However, the ground floor’s flooring may be uneven due to the building’s historical nature.

Interior Movement: The floors are often uneven, and some areas may be difficult to navigate, especially the monumental rooms, which feature intricate marble flooring. Staff are available to assist if needed.

Navigating the Library

While the ground floor and the first-floor rooms are generally accessible, the second floor and higher levels have narrow stairs and are not equipped with elevators. Access to the reading rooms and archives on higher levels is typically restricted to researchers or staff, and those areas are not wheelchair accessible.

Visiting Tips for Accessibility

Assistance: Staff are usually available to help visitors who require assistance, especially if there are mobility challenges. It's best to ask for help at the information desk.

Best Time to Visit: For a more comfortable visit, consider coming early in the day when the library is less crowded.

Accessible Restrooms

No Accessible Restrooms Inside: The library does not have wheelchair-accessible toilets within the building. The nearest accessible restrooms are in Piazza San Marco (public toilets are available for a fee).
