Marciana Library

Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana

Story

The Marciana Library (Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana) in Venice is one of the oldest and most important libraries in Italy, known for its vast collection of classical texts and stunning Renaissance architecture. Founded in the 16th century, it was envisioned as a grand repository for knowledge, fulfilling the wishes of Cardinal Bessarion, who donated his vast collection of Greek and Latin manuscripts to Venice in 1468. Construction began in 1537 under the renowned architect Jacopo Sansovino, and the library became a masterpiece of Venetian Renaissance design.

Located in Piazza San Marco, near the Doge’s Palace, it houses rare manuscripts, ancient maps, and works by famous scholars. Today, the Marciana Library remains a symbol of Venetian scholarship and artistic achievement..

A Gift of Knowledge

The library was founded after Cardinal Bessarion, a Greek scholar and former Archbishop of Constantinople, donated over 1,000 rare Greek and Latin manuscripts to Venice in 1468 to preserve classical knowledge.

Designed by a Renaissance Master

The library’s stunning architecture was created by Jacopo Sansovino, one of the greatest Renaissance architects, who also designed parts of St. Mark’s Square.

Home to Rare Treasures

Among its priceless collections, the library holds a 15th-century edition of Ptolemy’s "Geography," early printed books, and the world’s oldest surviving complete manuscript of Homer’s "Iliad."

A Secret Door

The library has a hidden passageway connecting it to the Doge’s Palace, allowing secret movement between the two buildings.

Center of Learning

The library continues to be a major center for historical and academic research, holding over a million books, including rare manuscripts and maps from Venice’s golden age.

Characters

Cardinal Bessarion: A Greek scholar and former Archbishop of Constantinople, Bessarion donated over 1,000 manuscripts in 1468. His collection became the foundation of the Marciana Library.

Jacopo Sansovino: Sansovino designed the stunning library building in 1537. His work set a new standard for Venetian Renaissance architecture.

Titian: The renowned Venetian painter Titian contributed to the library’s decoration, creating some of the ceiling frescoes.

Galileo Galilei: Galileo gave public lectures at here in the early 1600s, he demonstrated his telescope and presented his astronomical discoveries.

Tomaso Giannotti: Appointed in 1560, he became the first official librarian of the Marciana Library and helped organize its growing collection.

Elisabetta Caminer Turra: A pioneering Venetian female writer and publisher, Turra used the Marciana Library for research and is considered one of the first professional female journalists in Italy.

Floor/Area Guide

Ground Floor: Sansovino’s architecture, marble statues, entrance and bookshop.

First Floor: Frescoed Sala Sansoviniana, antique globes, and rare manuscripts.

Second Floor: Scholar-only rooms with Ptolemy’s Geography and historic archives.

Accessibility: Ground floor is accessible; upper floors not elevator-equipped.

Restrooms: No accessible toilets inside; public restrooms at Piazza San Marco.
